1. Admissibility of Evidence

The Goal of Investigation

The primary goal of any computer forensic investigation is to collect evidence using accepted methods that will withstand legal scrutiny. Evidence must be gathered in a manner that ensures its integrity and reliability so that it can be presented in a court of law.

For evidence to be admissible in court, it must meet several critical requirements:

Requirements for Evidence Admissibility

Judge's Acceptance

The judge must accept (admit) the evidence for it to be considered by the court. This decision is known as admission of evidence. Without the judge's approval, evidence cannot be presented to the jury or considered in the verdict.

Legal Search and Seizure

Evidence must be obtained through legal search and seizure procedures. This means investigators must have proper authorization (such as a warrant) before collecting evidence from a suspect's devices or premises.

Chain of Custody

A complete and unbroken chain of custody must be maintained from the moment evidence is collected until it is presented in court. Every person who handles the evidence must be documented.

Accepted Methods

Evidence collection and analysis must follow accepted forensic methods and use validated tools. Improperly collected evidence can be challenged and excluded from proceedings.

Critical Point: If any of these requirements are not met, the evidence may be deemed inadmissible, potentially allowing a guilty party to go free. The investigator's primary responsibility is to ensure all evidence meets the standards for admissibility.

2. Case Study: CD Universe Prosecution Failure

When Evidence Handling Goes Wrong

The CD Universe case is a cautionary tale about how improper evidence handling can destroy a prosecution, even when the suspect is identified.

The Case

CD Universe, an online music retailer, was the victim of an extortion attempt by a hacker known as "Maxim". The hacker had stolen credit card numbers from the company's database and demanded a ransom for their return.

What Went Wrong

The FBI investigation was compromised because investigators used the original data instead of creating a forensic copy. This critical error violated fundamental forensic procedures:

  • The original evidence was directly accessed and potentially altered
  • No forensic image (bit-by-bit copy) was created before analysis
  • The integrity of the evidence could not be verified
  • The chain of custody was effectively broken

Lesson Learned: This case demonstrates why investigators must always work with forensic copies, never with original data. The first rule of digital forensics is to preserve the original evidence in an unaltered state. Even law enforcement agencies can fail prosecutions when this principle is violated.

3. Digital Profiling

What is Digital Profiling?

Digital profiling is the process of using electronic evidence (e-evidence) to build a behavioral profile of a suspect. E-evidence supplies investigators with behavior patterns that can reveal a person's habits, intentions, communications, and activities.

Sources of E-Evidence for Profiling

Digital Profiling Evidence Sources

💻

Computer Data

Files, documents, browsing history, installed applications, system logs, and user activity records stored on computers and storage devices.

📧

Email

Email messages, attachments, headers, address books, and communication patterns that reveal relationships and intentions.

📱

Cell Phone

Call logs, text messages, GPS location data, app usage, and stored media from mobile devices that track movements and communications.

🔊

Wiretaps

Legally authorized interceptions of communications, including phone calls, internet traffic, and electronic messages.

Important: Digital profiling allows investigators to piece together a comprehensive picture of a suspect's activities, relationships, and motives. The combination of evidence from multiple sources creates a powerful investigative tool that can either support or refute allegations.

4. Crimes Solved Using Forensics

Digital forensics has played a crucial role in solving many high-profile criminal cases. The following table highlights notable cases where electronic evidence was instrumental in achieving justice:

Criminal Crime E-Evidence Used
Dennis Rader Serial killer (BTK Killer) Metadata from a floppy disk sent to police traced back to his church computer, revealing his identity after decades of evasion.
Lee Boyd Malvo / John Allen Muhammad D.C. Sniper attacks Laptop computer evidence, phone records, and GPS data linked the suspects to the series of sniper shootings.
Lisa Montgomery Murder and kidnapping Internet search history showed research on childbirth and cesarean procedures, establishing premeditation.
David Westerfield Kidnapping and murder Computer forensics revealed internet browsing history containing child pornography, supporting the prosecution's case.
Scott Peterson Murder of wife and unborn child Computer evidence including internet searches for boat launch sites and ocean currents in the area where the body was found.
Alejandro Avila Kidnapping and murder Digital evidence from computers and electronic devices helped establish the suspect's movements and intent.
Zacarias Moussaoui Terrorism (9/11 conspiracy) Laptop evidence containing flight simulation software, aviation training materials, and communications with other conspirators.

These cases demonstrate that digital evidence can be decisive in both identifying suspects and proving their guilt. From metadata on floppy disks to internet search histories, the digital footprints we leave behind can serve as powerful evidence in criminal investigations.

5. Forensics Investigation Methods

Investigation Objectives

A forensic investigation follows specific methods designed to comprehensively examine digital evidence while maintaining its integrity. Each objective serves a specific purpose in building a complete picture of the evidence.

Key Objectives of Forensic Investigation

Forensic Investigation Objectives

1. Protect the Suspect's System

Ensure the suspect's computer system is not altered or damaged during the investigation. Use write-blockers and work only with forensic copies.

2. Discover All Files

Locate and catalog all files on the system, including active files, system files, and application files.

3. Recover Deleted Files

Use forensic tools to recover files that have been deleted by the user but may still exist on the storage media.

4. Reveal Hidden Files

Identify files that have been intentionally hidden through operating system features, file attribute manipulation, or other concealment methods.

Advanced Investigation Objectives

5. Access Encrypted Files

Attempt to decrypt password-protected or encrypted files using forensic decryption tools and techniques.

6. Steganalysis

Detect and extract data hidden within other files (such as images, audio, or video) using steganographic techniques.

7. Analyze Unallocated and Slack Space

Examine areas of the disk not currently assigned to active files, which may contain remnants of deleted or overwritten data.

8. Print Analysis and Expert Testimony

Prepare printouts and reports of findings, and be prepared to provide expert testimony in court explaining the evidence and analysis methods.

Important: The investigator must follow all these objectives systematically. Missing any step could leave critical evidence undiscovered, potentially affecting the outcome of the case.

6. Unallocated Space and File Slack

Unallocated Space

Unallocated space (also called free space) is the area on a hard drive that is not currently assigned to any file by the file system. When a file is deleted, the space it occupied is marked as unallocated, but the actual data may still remain on the disk until it is overwritten by new data.

File Slack

File slack is the unused space within a file cluster that exists between the end of the actual file data and the end of the cluster. Because files rarely fill an entire cluster perfectly, the remaining space (slack) may contain data fragments from previously stored files.

Types of File Slack

  • RAM Slack: The space between the end of the file and the end of the last sector used by the file. This area may be filled with data from system memory (RAM).
  • Drive Slack: The remaining sectors in the cluster that are not used by the file. These sectors may contain data from previously deleted files.

Why Are These Important for Investigators?

  • Unallocated space often contains recoverable deleted files and data fragments
  • File slack can reveal remnants of previously stored data that the user may have tried to destroy
  • Together, they can provide critical evidence that is invisible to normal users and standard file browsing tools
  • Forensic tools like EnCase and FTK can systematically search and analyze both unallocated space and file slack
  • This data may include deleted emails, documents, images, or other files relevant to the investigation

A common misconception is that deleting a file removes it permanently. In reality, the file system simply marks the space as available for reuse. The actual data persists until overwritten, making forensic recovery possible and these areas a goldmine for investigators.

7. Challenges to Evidence

Suppression Hearings

Defense attorneys may challenge the admissibility of digital evidence through suppression hearings. In these hearings, the defense argues that certain evidence should be excluded from trial because it was obtained improperly or in violation of the defendant's rights.

Common Grounds for Challenging Evidence

  • Illegal search and seizure: Evidence obtained without proper authorization (warrant) or in violation of the Fourth Amendment
  • Broken chain of custody: Gaps or inconsistencies in the documentation of who handled the evidence
  • Improper collection methods: Use of non-standard or unvalidated forensic tools and techniques
  • Evidence contamination: Working with original evidence instead of forensic copies, or failure to use write-blockers
  • Lack of qualification: The forensic examiner may lack proper credentials or training

Ethical Procedures Are Essential

Investigators must follow ethical procedures at every stage of the investigation. Any deviation from accepted practices can provide grounds for evidence suppression. This includes obtaining proper authorization, using validated tools, maintaining chain of custody, and conducting analyses objectively without bias.

Key Point: The best evidence in the world is worthless if it cannot be admitted in court. Forensic investigators must be as meticulous about their procedures as they are about their technical analysis.

8. Search Warrants

What is a Search Warrant?

A search warrant is a legal document issued by a judge or magistrate that authorizes law enforcement to search a specific location for specific evidence. In digital forensics, warrants are required to search computers, mobile devices, cloud accounts, and other digital storage.

Key Components of a Search Warrant

  • Affidavit: A sworn statement by the investigator that presents facts and evidence to justify the search. The affidavit must demonstrate probable cause -- a reasonable belief that evidence of a crime will be found at the specified location.
  • Probable Cause: The legal standard that must be met before a warrant is issued. The investigator must show that there is a reasonable basis to believe that the search will uncover evidence related to a crime.
  • Specificity: The warrant must specifically describe the place to be searched and the items to be seized. Overly broad warrants may be challenged and invalidated.

Exceptions to Warrant Requirement

There are certain circumstances where a search may be conducted without a warrant:

  • Consent: The owner or authorized user voluntarily agrees to the search
  • Plain view: Evidence is in plain view during a lawful observation
  • Exigent circumstances: Emergency situations where evidence may be destroyed if not immediately seized
  • Search incident to arrest: A limited search conducted at the time of a lawful arrest
  • Border searches: Special rules apply at international borders and customs checkpoints

Fourth Amendment Protection

The Fourth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution protects individuals from unreasonable searches and seizures. Evidence obtained in violation of this amendment may be subject to the exclusionary rule, meaning it cannot be used in court. Forensic investigators must understand and respect these constitutional protections.

9. A Terrorist's Trial: The Moussaoui Case

Case Background

Zacarias Moussaoui was arrested in August 2001 on immigration charges. He had been attending flight school in Minnesota under suspicious circumstances, and his laptop computer was seized upon arrest.

The FBI's Dilemma

Before September 11, 2001, the FBI was denied permission to search Moussaoui's laptop. The request for a warrant was rejected because the legal standard for probable cause was deemed insufficient at the time.

September 11 Aftermath

After the devastating attacks of September 11, 2001, the significance of Moussaoui's case became clear:

  • His laptop contained flight simulation software and aviation materials
  • Communications with other suspected terrorists were discovered
  • Evidence suggested he was aware of the planned attacks
  • He was eventually convicted as a co-conspirator in the 9/11 attacks

Lesson Learned: This case highlighted the tension between civil liberties and national security. It contributed to significant changes in surveillance and search authority laws, including the passage of the USA PATRIOT Act, which expanded law enforcement's ability to obtain warrants for terrorism-related investigations.

10. Motives for Cybercrimes

Understanding the motives behind cybercrimes helps investigators anticipate what evidence to look for and where to find it. The primary motives include:

Common Motives for Cybercrimes

💰

Financial Gain

The most common motive. Includes fraud, theft of financial data, ransomware attacks, and unauthorized access to bank accounts or payment systems.

🚫

Cover Up

Using digital methods to conceal other criminal activities, such as altering records, destroying logs, or creating false digital trails.

🗑

Remove Evidence

Deleting, encrypting, or destroying digital evidence of crimes to avoid detection and prosecution.

🔒

Steal Information

Unauthorized access to steal intellectual property, trade secrets, personal data, or classified information.

🏭

Industrial Espionage

Corporate spying to gain competitive advantage by stealing proprietary information, research data, or business strategies from rival companies.

Investigative Insight: Identifying the motive helps narrow the scope of the investigation. For instance, if financial gain is suspected, investigators will focus on financial records, transaction logs, and banking-related evidence. If industrial espionage is the motive, the focus shifts to proprietary documents and external communications.

11. Categories of Cybercrimes

Cybercrimes can be categorized based on the role the computer plays in the criminal activity. Understanding these categories helps investigators determine the appropriate forensic approach.

Three Categories of Cybercrimes

1. Computer as Target

The computer or network itself is the target of the crime. Examples include hacking, denial-of-service attacks, malware distribution, unauthorized access, and website defacement. The criminal directly attacks the computer system.

2. Computer as Instrument

The computer is used as a tool to commit the crime. Examples include online fraud, identity theft, phishing schemes, digital forgery, and cyber harassment. The computer is the means by which the crime is carried out.

3. Computer as Incidental

The computer is not central to the crime but contains evidence related to it. Examples include a drug dealer's customer list stored on a computer, financial records of money laundering, or communications planning a physical crime.

The category of cybercrime determines the forensic approach. When the computer is the target, network forensics and system analysis are primary. When it is the instrument, the focus is on the specific applications and data used. When incidental, the goal is to extract relevant evidence from among large volumes of unrelated data.

12. Chain of Custody Procedures

The Three C's

Proper evidence management is built on three fundamental principles known as the Three C's: Care, Control, and Chain of Custody. These principles ensure that evidence remains authentic, unaltered, and admissible throughout the entire investigation process.

The Three C's Explained

The Three C's of Evidence Management

Care

Evidence must be handled with the utmost care to prevent damage, contamination, or alteration. This includes using proper anti-static protection, storing media in appropriate conditions, and using write-blocking devices when accessing digital evidence.

Control

Evidence must be under strict control at all times. Access must be limited to authorized personnel only. The evidence room must be secured, and every access must be logged and documented.

Chain of Custody

A complete and unbroken record of who has had possession of the evidence, when they received it, what they did with it, and when they transferred it to the next person. This chain must be documented from collection through court presentation.

Evidence Log Requirements

An evidence log must contain the following information for each piece of evidence:

Log Element Description
Case Number Unique identifier for the case
Evidence Description Detailed description of the evidence item (make, model, serial number)
Date/Time Collected Exact date and time the evidence was collected
Collected By Name and badge/ID number of the person who collected the evidence
Location Found Precise location where the evidence was found
Transfer Records Each transfer including date, time, from whom, to whom, and purpose

Mirror-Image Copies

A critical part of chain of custody procedures is the creation of mirror-image copies (forensic images) of all digital evidence. These are exact, bit-by-bit copies of the original storage media that include:

  • All active files and folders
  • Deleted files and file fragments
  • Unallocated space
  • File slack space
  • System files and metadata

Critical Rule: All forensic analysis must be performed on the mirror-image copy, never on the original evidence. The original must be preserved in a secure location in its unaltered state.

13. Report Procedures

Objectivity in Reporting

Forensic reports must be completely objective. The investigator's role is to present facts only -- what was found, how it was found, and what the evidence shows. The report must not contain opinions on guilt or innocence.

Report Writing Guidelines

  • Present facts, not opinions: Describe what the evidence shows without drawing conclusions about guilt
  • Use precise language: Avoid ambiguous terms; be specific about dates, times, file names, and locations
  • Document methodology: Explain the tools and techniques used for analysis
  • Include all findings: Report both inculpatory (incriminating) and exculpatory (exonerating) evidence
  • Be reproducible: Another qualified examiner should be able to follow your steps and reach the same findings
  • Use clear organization: Structure the report logically with an executive summary, methodology, findings, and conclusions

No Opinions on Guilt

The forensic investigator is a neutral expert, not an advocate for either side. Reports must never state or imply whether the suspect is guilty or innocent. This is the role of the judge and jury, not the forensic examiner. Expressing opinions on guilt can undermine the credibility of the entire investigation.

14. Investigator's Responsibilities

A computer forensic investigator carries significant responsibilities that extend beyond technical analysis. These responsibilities ensure the integrity of the investigation and the admissibility of evidence.

Core Responsibilities

  • Review crimes thoroughly: Understand the nature of the alleged crime and what evidence might be relevant
  • Maintain objectivity: Approach every investigation without preconceptions or bias, examining all evidence fairly
  • Follow established procedures: Adhere to accepted forensic methodologies and organizational protocols at all times
  • Keep detailed logs: Document every action taken during the investigation, including tools used, commands executed, and observations made
  • Protect evidence integrity: Ensure evidence is never altered, damaged, or contaminated
  • Stay current: Continuously update knowledge of forensic tools, techniques, and legal requirements
  • Maintain professional ethics: Conduct investigations honestly, impartially, and within the bounds of the law
  • Prepare for testimony: Be ready to explain findings and methods in court in a clear and understandable manner

The investigator serves as the bridge between the digital evidence and the legal system. Their competence, integrity, and professionalism directly affect whether justice is achieved. A single procedural error or ethical lapse can compromise an entire case.
