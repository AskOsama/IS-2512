1. What is Computer Forensics?

Definition

Computer Forensics is the search for, and the collection of, evidence from computer systems in a standardized and well-documented manner to maintain its admissibility and probative value in a legal proceeding.

Computer forensics involves:

  • Identification: Recognizing potential sources of digital evidence
  • Preservation: Protecting evidence from alteration or destruction
  • Collection: Gathering evidence using proper techniques
  • Analysis: Examining data to find relevant information
  • Documentation: Recording all findings and processes
  • Presentation: Reporting results in a legally acceptable manner

2. Computer Forensics is NOT Hacking

Critical Legal Warning

Computer forensics must be conducted within strict legal boundaries. The following activities are illegal and can result in felony charges:

  • Never use "Spy-ware"
  • Never "hack" a password
  • Never login to an account unauthorized (without a warrant or court order)
  • Keystroke loggers are no longer legal without proper authorization

Important: All of these violations are now (usually) a felony. The law is in flux, so always stay updated on current legal requirements. When in doubt, consult legal counsel before proceeding with any investigation.

The Ethical Investigator

A computer forensic investigator must:

  • Obtain proper legal authorization before accessing any system
  • Follow established protocols and procedures
  • Document every action taken during an investigation
  • Maintain the integrity of all evidence
  • Never alter or destroy original evidence

3. Introduction to E-Evidence

Criminal investigations involve the analysis of various types of evidence:

  • Ballistic or bloodstain patterns
  • Gunpowder residue
  • Tire tracks
  • Fingerprints
  • Evidence left by electronic devices

E-Evidence

E-evidence (Electronic Evidence) is the digital equivalent of the physical evidence found at crime scenes. It provides crucial information about activities, communications, and intentions.

The Digital Landscape

  • The expansion of the Internet provides countless opportunities for crimes to be committed
  • Digital technologies record and document electronic trails of information that can be analyzed later

Sources of Electronic Trails:

  • E-mail communications
  • Instant messages (IM)
  • Web site visits and browsing history
  • PDAs and mobile devices
  • Smart phones
  • Cookies and log files

4. Basics of Crimes

Early cases illustrate the importance of knowing the law regarding computer crimes:

Case Study: Morris Worm (1988)

  • Robert T. Morris Jr. created one of the first recognized worms
  • Morris was charged with violation of the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA)
  • Sentenced to: 3 years probation, 400 hours of community service, and a $10,500 fine

Case Study: Lovebug Virus (2000)

  • Created by Onel De Guzman from the Philippines
  • Caused approximately $7 billion in damage worldwide
  • De Guzman was released because no law in the Philippines made what he had done a crime at that time

Key Insight: Computer crimes can be prosecuted only if they violate existing laws. This highlights the importance of having up-to-date cybercrime legislation.

Definition of Crime

A crime is an offensive act against society that violates a law and is punishable by the government.

Two important principles in this definition:

  1. The act must violate at least one criminal law
  2. It is the government (not the victim of the crime) that punishes the violator

5. Crime Categories and Sentencing

Crimes are divided into two broad categories:

Category Severity Punishment
Felonies Serious crimes Fine and more than one year in prison
Misdemeanors Lesser crimes Fine and less than one year in prison

The severity of punishment for computer crimes depends on factors such as the amount of damage caused, the number of victims, and whether the perpetrator has prior offenses.

6. Cybercrime Categories

The following terms are used interchangeably:

  • Computer crime
  • Cybercrime
  • Information crime
  • High-tech crime

Two Categories of Computer-Related Offenses

Computer Crime Categories

Computer as Target

The computer or its data is the target of the crime. Examples: hacking, data theft, malware attacks, denial of service.

Computer as Instrument

The computer is used as a tool to commit the crime. Examples: fraud, identity theft, online harassment, distribution of illegal content.

Information Warfare and Cyberterrorism

Information warfare is the extension of war into and through cyberspace. It includes attacks on critical infrastructure, espionage, and disruption of communications.

8. Evidence Basics and Types

What is Evidence?

Evidence is proof of a fact about what did or did not happen.

Three Types of Evidence for Persuasion

  • Testimony of a witness - Verbal or written statements from individuals
  • Physical evidence - Tangible items that can be collected and examined
  • Electronic evidence - Digital data stored on or transmitted by electronic devices

Important: Both cybercrimes and traditional crimes can leave cybertrails of evidence.

Types of Evidence

Evidence Classification

🔍

Artifact Evidence

Change in evidence that causes investigator to think the evidence relates to the crime

⚖️

Inculpatory Evidence

Evidence that supports a given theory (points toward guilt)

🛡️

Exculpatory Evidence

Evidence that contradicts a given theory (points toward innocence)

✓

Admissible Evidence

Evidence allowed to be presented at trial

✗

Inadmissible Evidence

Evidence that cannot be presented at trial

⚠️

Tainted Evidence

Evidence obtained from illegal search or seizure

Additional Evidence Types

  • Circumstantial evidence: Shows circumstances that logically lead to a conclusion of fact
  • Hearsay evidence: Secondhand evidence (testimony about what someone else said)
  • Material evidence: Evidence relevant and significant to the lawsuit
  • Immaterial evidence: Evidence that is not relevant or significant

9. Electronic Evidence: Technology and Legal Issues

Key Characteristics of Electronic Evidence

  • Volatile: Electronic evidence may be easily changed
  • Persistent: Conversely, it is difficult to delete entirely
  • Abundant: E-mail evidence has become the most common type of e-evidence

Search Warrant Requirements

For evidence to be admissible, proper legal procedures must be followed:

  • A search warrant is issued only if law enforcement provides sufficient proof that there is probable cause a crime has been committed
  • The law officer must specify what premises, things, or persons will be searched
  • Evidence discovered during the search can be seized

Real Case: Forensics Saves a Life (2004)

In 2004, Bobbie Jo Stinnett was murdered and her unborn baby "kidnapped." Police examined her computer and traced an IP address to Lisa Montgomery, who had corresponded with Stinnett over the Internet. This case demonstrates how computer forensics can be crucial in solving violent crimes.

10. Importance of Computer Forensics

Computer Forensics Investigations Supply Evidence For:

Criminal Cases:

  • Homicide investigations
  • Financial fraud
  • Drug-related crimes
  • Child exploitation cases

Civil Cases:

  • Fraud claims
  • Divorce proceedings
  • Discrimination lawsuits
  • Harassment cases

Computer forensics is also used to prevent, detect, and respond to cyber attacks.

Largest Computer Forensics Case in History: Enron

  • Government investigators searched more than 400 computers and handheld devices
  • Over 10,000 backup tapes were examined
  • The investigation included records from Arthur Andersen, Enron's accounting firm
  • "Explosive" e-mail from J.P. Morgan Chase employees about Enron was part of a corollary case

What Computer Forensics Can Reveal:

  • Theft of intellectual property, trade secrets, confidential data
  • Defamatory or revealing statements in chat rooms, usenet groups, or IM
  • Sending of harassing, hateful, or other objectionable e-mail
  • Downloading of criminally pornographic material
  • Downloading or installation of unlicensed software
  • Online gambling, insider trading, solicitation, drug trafficking
  • Files accessed, altered, or saved

What Computer Forensics Can Recover:

  • Lost client records intentionally deleted by an employee
  • Proof that an ex-employee stole company trade secrets
  • Proof of violations of noncompete agreements
  • Proof that a supplier's information security negligence caused costly mistakes
  • Proof of a safer design of a defective item in a product liability suit
  • Earlier drafts of sensitive documents or altered spreadsheets to prove intent in a fraud claim

11. Discovery Process and Methods

Discovery Process

Discovery is the pretrial right of each party to "discover" or learn about the opponent's case. It includes information that must be provided by each party if requested.

Methods of Discovery

Four Discovery Methods

Interrogatories

Written answers made under oath to written questions

Requests for Admissions

Intended to ascertain the authenticity of a document or the truth of an assertion

Requests for Production

Involves the inspection of documents and property

Depositions

Out-of-court testimony made under oath by the opposing party or other witnesses

Increased Demand for E-Discovery

  • Most business operations and transactions are done on computers and stored on digital devices
  • Most common means of communication are electronic
  • People are candid in their e-mail and instant messages
  • E-evidence is very difficult to destroy

13. Challenges and Deletion Fallacies

Challenges in Computer Forensics

  • Evidence collection is done in an adversarial environment
  • Judge and jury are not technical
  • Commercial testing tools may not work in all situations

Common "Deletion" / "Obfuscation" Fallacies

Myth vs. Reality

Myth: "I delete the file, it's gone"

Reality: Deleted files are recoverable using digital forensics tools

Myth: "I changed the name of the file, now no one can find it"

Reality: Digital forensics tools immediately identify files based on content - names do not matter at all

Myth: "I formatted the drive"

Reality: This destroys almost nothing - data can still be recovered

Myth: "I use only web-based email"

Reality: Some email fragments are still present locally in browser cache and temporary files

Summary and Key Takeaways

What We've Learned

  1. E-evidence plays an important role in crime reconstruction and is the digital equivalent of physical evidence.
  2. Crimes are not limited to cybercrimes; cyber trails are left by many traditional crimes as well.
  3. Without evidence of an act or activity that violates a statute, there is no crime. This is why understanding laws is crucial for forensic investigators.
  4. Rules must be followed to gather, search for, and seize evidence in order to protect individual rights and ensure admissibility.
  5. Computer forensics is NOT hacking. Investigators must operate within strict legal boundaries.
  6. Three skill sets are essential: Legal Procedures, Computer Technology, and Investigative Techniques.
  7. E-discovery refers to the discovery of electronic documents, data, e-mail, etc., and is more complex than traditional discovery.
  8. Tools used to recover lost or destroyed data can also be used in e-discovery of evidence.
  9. Common deletion fallacies show that data is rarely truly destroyed, making forensic recovery possible in most cases.
